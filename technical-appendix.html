MANDEVAL Technical Appendix

This appendix explains how the MANDEVAL tool organises cost and benefit inputs. It is written so that you can plug in your own best estimates and update them over time. No values are hard coded in the tool; all defaults can be replaced by country teams.

1. Cost parameters

The tool keeps the cost structure deliberately simple and transparent. All costs are entered in the same currency, usually per year for a given mandate design:

Cost item Examples How MANDEVAL uses it
Administration and delivery Staff time to administer mandates, maintain registers, update IT systems, respond to queries and issue certificates or QR codes. Entered as a single amount in the Costs tab and added directly to total cost.
Communication and outreach Public information campaigns, hotline services, community engagement, translation and accessible formats. Entered as a single amount and added to total cost. You can separate recurring and one-off activities in your own costing sheet if useful.
Enforcement and compliance Inspection, compliance checks, appeals, legal and administrative procedures related to non-compliance. Entered as a single amount and added to total cost. It can be set to zero if enforcement is minimal or handled under existing budgets.
Compensation and support Compensation schemes for vaccine injury, hardship support for people who lose work because of mandates, and costs of processing exemption requests. Entered as a single amount and added to total cost. It can be treated as a pooled fund if individual payments are uncertain.

The tool calculates total cost as the sum of these four components. Countries can align each item with a more detailed costing template if they wish.

2. Benefit parameters

MANDEVAL focuses on one main health outcome: lives saved due to higher vaccine coverage under a mandate compared with a reference situation. Users provide the key inputs:

Benefit item Definition How MANDEVAL uses it
Lives saved per 100,000 Expected reduction in deaths per 100,000 people in the covered population due to the mandate. This should come from your own epidemiological modelling or external evidence. Combined with the population covered in the configuration step to estimate total lives saved for the scenario.
Monetary value per life saved The monetary value used by your country team for one life saved. This may be based on a value of a statistical life (VSL), value of a life year (VOLY) or other national guidance on valuing mortality reductions. Multiplied by total lives saved to obtain the total monetary benefit for the scenario. If you prefer not to monetise lives saved, you can set this to zero and focus on natural units.
Indicative public support A simple 0–100 index representing expected public support or acceptability for the mandate design. This can come from survey data, preference studies or expert judgement. Used for traffic-light signalling and ranking. It does not enter the benefit–cost ratio directly but helps distinguish otherwise similar scenarios.

3. Derived indicators

Once costs and benefits are entered, the tool computes:

  • Total lives saved as lives saved per 100,000 multiplied by the population covered in the scenario.
  • Total monetary benefit as total lives saved multiplied by the monetary value per life saved.
  • Benefit–cost ratio (BCR) as total monetary benefit divided by total cost, when both are positive.

Countries can adjust the underlying values over time as new evidence emerges. The purpose of the tool is to make these assumptions visible and easy to modify, not to embed any fixed global benchmark.
