Decision Aid Tool for Vaccine Mandate Design (Australia, France, Italy)

Version: v1.2.0 Built: Jan 2026

What this tool does

eMANDEVAL-Future helps governments and public health agencies explore the implications of different potential future vaccine mandates in Australia, France and Italy. It combines:

  • Preference evidence from a discrete choice experiment in the three countries.
  • Simple epidemiological inputs (expected lives saved per 100,000 people).
  • Indicative implementation costs and a benefit metric (for example a value of statistical life).
  • A lightweight distributional ‚Äúequity lens‚Äù captured as qualitative notes (without turning this into a full equity model).

The tool summarises trade-offs between predicted public support, lives saved and implementation costs. It does not replace legal, ethical or political judgement.

How to use this tool

  1. Settings: Specify the analysis horizon, population covered, currency label and benefit metric (for example a value of statistical life) with a default value.
  2. Configuration: Choose a country, outbreak scenario and mandate design (scope, exemptions, coverage threshold, expected lives saved). Add distributional notes if relevant.
  3. Costing: Enter indicative implementation costs across major cost categories or load evidence-based defaults for the selected country and scenario.
  4. Results: Review predicted public support, lives saved, benefit‚Äìcost metrics and lives-saved-equivalent preferences (MRS), including visual summaries and an equity flag if notes were recorded.
  5. Saved scenarios: Save and export scenarios for comparison and reporting. Pin up to 3 scenarios for dashboard comparison.
  6. Briefings & AI prompts: Generate briefing text and structured prompts for Copilot or ChatGPT, including comparisons across selected scenarios and an explicit equity reflection block.

Data and methods

Model-based public support is derived from mixed logit estimates of preferences for potential future vaccine mandate designs in Australia, France and Italy under mild and severe outbreak frames. Support is computed as the probability of choosing a mandate over an opt-out option, averaged over 1,000 draws from the estimated preference distributions (fixed seed for reproducibility).

Cost and benefit inputs are indicative and should be aligned with country-specific regulatory impact analysis guidance before formal use.

This tool summarises preference and cost‚Äìbenefit evidence for potential future vaccine mandates. It does not replace legal review, ethical assessment or detailed equity analysis.

Mandate design attributes in the preference study

The discrete choice experiment asked members of the public in Australia, France and Italy to choose between potential future vaccine mandate options and an opt-out (no mandate) option. Each mandate was described using the attributes below.

üè• Scope of mandate i

  • High-risk occupations only
    Vaccination is required only for people in jobs with high exposure risk or essential public roles (for example healthcare workers, emergency responders).
  • All occupations & public spaces
    Everyone working or entering public venues (for example cinemas, malls, restaurants, public transport) must be vaccinated.

üìú Exemption policy i

  • Medical only
    Only those with certified medical reasons (for example severe allergies) can opt out of vaccination.
  • Medical + religious
    Exemptions are allowed for medical reasons or documented religious beliefs (with verification).
  • Medical + religious + personal belief
    Exemptions are allowed for medical reasons, religious beliefs or strong personal objections (broader opt-out).

üéØ Coverage requirement to lift mandate i

  • 50% population vaccinated
    The mandate is lifted once about half of the population has been vaccinated.
  • 70% population vaccinated
    The mandate is lifted once most people are vaccinated and near-normal life can resume.
  • 90% population vaccinated
    The mandate is lifted once almost everyone is vaccinated and it is considered safe to remove all mandates.

‚ù§Ô∏è Expected lives saved i

This attribute summarises the expected epidemiological benefit from introducing a mandate, expressed as lives saved per 100,000 people.

  • 10 per 100,000 — saves about 10 lives per 100,000 people.
  • 25 per 100,000 — saves about 25 lives per 100,000 people.
  • 35 per 100,000 — saves about 35 lives per 100,000 people.
  • 40 per 100,000 — saves about 40 lives per 100,000 people.

In the tool, this appears as a slider from 0‚Äì100 per 100,000 people, but the realistic range for the underlying study is 10‚Äì40 per 100,000.

Lives-saved equivalents (MRS)

The table below interprets preferences for mandate features on a ‚Äúlives saved per 100,000 people‚Äù scale. Each value shows how many expected lives saved per 100,000 people would be equivalent, in terms of acceptability, to the change in the mandate feature.

Change in mandate design Lives-saved equivalent (per 100,000) Interpretation

Configure a mandate to see how changes in scope, exemptions or coverage compare to changes in expected lives saved. Positive values indicate changes that make the option less preferred; negative values indicate changes that make it more preferred.

Lives-saved equivalents ‚Äì visual summary

Bars above zero indicate changes that require more expected lives saved per 100,000 people to maintain the same acceptability (less preferred). Bars below zero indicate changes that people view as more desirable.

Results are based on stated-preference data and simple cost‚Äìbenefit calculations. They provide structured evidence but do not by themselves determine whether a mandate is appropriate.
